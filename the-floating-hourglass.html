Introduction

We started by looking at the following animation:

This is very counter intuitive. Though the distribution of mass changes within the hourglass, the total mass and volume is always the same.

In physics we are taught that for Newton's 2nd Law, , we can treat distributed masses like a point, with all of the forces acting at the center of mass. So it seems that the force of gravity and the buoyant force should always be the same. This would imply that the hourglass either always sinks, or always floats since the sum of the external forces is not changing. The odd feeling that this isn't behaving as expected prompted the podcast.

Derek's Hypothesis

Derek came up with a theory about how the hourglass could wedge itself against the walls of the container due to an unstable equilibrium. We know from the start of the GIF that the hourglass wants to float, and therefore it should always float unless another force is acting on it.

Unlike Newton's 2nd Law, when we consider the net torque on a body we can not treat it like a point mass. Thus how the mass is distributed will effect the torque. When the sand is in the upper half, the hourglass would like to flip. But since the walls of the cylinder are preventing it from flipping, it gets stuck. The walls add another force to the system, friction. This frictional force holds the hourglass down prevents it from floating.

As the sand drains to the bottom, the hourglass will right itself vertically, and release itself from the wall. Now the force of friction is gone and the hourglass is free to float again.

As it turn's out, this is the correct answer.

Conclusion

From our two can setup we can draw some conclusions. Namely, that Newton was right and objects can be treated like point objects. We can see that we do meet the first half of \eqref{eq:kappacond} but we never meet the second half. This can be seen in the plot beneath the simulation. The sum of the the forces is always a constant value. It won't every start at zero and go to something else.

You should have found that only when did the cans move in the same direction at the same time. For all other situations, the right can starts going down and at some point, will begin to go up while the left can will do the opposite. Meeting the first half of \eqref{eq:kappacond}. But we can never change the net force, as predicted by Newton.

Derek suggested an unstable equilibrium could add an additional frictional force that keeps the hourglass in place.

Setup

Expectation

We know from the Two Can model above that whenever the top half of the hourglass will want to sink and the bottom will float for some time, then when reaches a particular value, they will switch.

This creates an unstable equilibrium that transforms into a stable one by the shifting of sand. When it becomes stable it does not want to rotate anymore and should rise.

Based on our observations of the GIF above, it looks like when the level of the sand on the top has decreased by about 25%. Thus we will calculate a value for when the equilibrium switches from unstable to stable, and see if the level of the sand in teh top half has decreased by 25%.

Intuition

Here we are only showing net forces. Notice on the picture above how the top has a net force acting downward and is to the right of the vertical (black dotted line). For the bottom we see the net force is upward and it is acting to the left of the vertical. If you imagine that the hourglass weren't bound by walls and you pushed on it it the way the forces are showing, it would spin clockwise.

Now when you add the walls they will exert the green forces on the hourglass. This normal force will also come with the yellow friction forces between the side of the wall and the hour glass. It is this yellow force that keeps the hourglass in place.

Thus if the hourglass is slightly off kilter, it will want to flip over.
